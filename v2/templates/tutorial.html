What Is Drug Discovery?

Traditional pharmaceutical research takes over a decade and costs between $1–2.6 billion per approved drug — and 90% of clinical candidates still fail. The primary causes: weak binding, toxicity, and poor pharmacokinetics identified far too late in the process.

The Drug Development Pipeline — Where It Breaks

StageObjectivePrimary Failure Reason
Hit DiscoveryFind molecules that interact with the targetLow selectivity or weak binding
Lead OptimizationRefine potency and safetyEmerging toxicity or poor metabolic profile
PreclinicalValidate in cell lines and animal modelsAnimal-to-human discrepancy
Phase I–III TrialsAssess safety and efficacy in humansLack of clinical efficacy (40–50% of failures)

How Deep2Lead Accelerates This

  1. You provide a seed molecule (SMILES string) and a protein target (amino acid sequence)
  2. Gemma4 (LLM on NVIDIA DGX GPU) generates novel structurally-similar candidate SMILES
  3. RDKit computes QED, SAS, LogP, MW, Tanimoto similarity, and Lipinski compliance for each
  4. A heuristic DTI model estimates drug-target binding probability
  5. Candidates are ranked by composite score — before any lab synthesis is required
  6. You publish findings to the class feed for peer review and scientific discussion

Chemistry Fundamentals — Atoms & Chemical Bonds

Drug-target binding is fundamentally about electron interactions. A drug molecule (the "key") must fit into a binding pocket on a protein (the "lock") through a series of electronic attractions — shape and charge together determine binding strength.

Key Atoms in Drug Molecules

C
Carbon
4 bonds · backbone of all organic molecules
N
Nitrogen
3 bonds · H-bond donor/acceptor · in proteins & DNA
O
Oxygen
2 bonds · H-bond acceptor · in water, drugs
H
Hydrogen
1 bond · H-bond donor when attached to N or O
S
Sulfur
2 bonds · in cysteine & methionine residues
Cl
Chlorine
1 bond · common halogen in drugs (e.g. Chlorambucil)

The Four Bond Types — Strength Determines Drug Behavior

Bond TypeRelative StrengthReversible?Role in Drugs
Covalent⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Extremely strongNo (usually)Rare — used in irreversible inhibitors; dangerous if off-target
Ionic⭐⭐⭐⭐ StrongYesSalt bridges in binding pockets
Hydrogen Bond⭐⭐⭐ ModerateYesThe workhorse of drug binding — N–H···O and O–H···N interactions
Van der Waals⭐⭐ WeakYesCollectively powerful when molecule fits snugly into a hydrophobic pocket
Key Insight: Most drugs bind using multiple weak, reversible forces simultaneously (hydrogen bonds + Van der Waals). This allows the drug to bind, trigger a signaling change, and then safely depart. Aspirin is unusual — it covalently modifies COX-1's Ser530, making it irreversible and explaining its long-lasting antiplatelet effect.

Molecular Properties & Scoring

Every generated candidate is evaluated on 6 molecular properties and ranked by a composite score. Understanding these scores lets you identify the most promising molecules from your results.

QED
Quantitative Estimate of Drug-likeness
Range: 0.0 → 1.0
Ideal: > 0.5  ·  Higher = more drug-like
A holistic score combining MW, LogP, H-bond donors/acceptors, polar surface area, and rotatable bonds. Aspirin scores ~0.61. Imatinib scores ~0.55.
SAS
Synthetic Accessibility Score
Range: 1.0 → 10.0
Ideal: < 4.0  ·  Lower = easier to synthesize
Estimates how difficult it would be to synthesize the molecule. Score is inverted in composite scoring — lower SAS means higher contribution to the final score.
LogP
Octanol-Water Partition Coefficient
Typical range: -3 to +7
Ideal: 0 to 3 for oral bioavailability
Too high = poor water solubility (can't dissolve in blood). Too low = can't cross lipid membranes. The sweet spot is 0–3 for most oral drugs.
MW
Molecular Weight
Units: Daltons (Da)
Ideal: < 500 Da (Lipinski Rule)
Larger molecules struggle to cross cell membranes. Aspirin = 180 Da, Imatinib = 493 Da (near the limit). Anything over 500 Da is flagged as a Lipinski violation.
Tanimoto
Tanimoto Similarity to Seed Molecule
Range: 0.0 → 1.0
1.0 = identical  ·  < 0.3 = very different
Computed using Morgan ECFP4 fingerprints (2048-bit). Ensures candidates are structurally relevant analogs of your seed. Values of 0.35–0.65 are typical for useful analogs.
Lipinski
Lipinski's Rule of Five
Result: Pass ✓ or Fail ✗
All 4 rules must pass for oral bioavailability
MW ≤ 500 Da · LogP ≤ 5 · H-bond donors ≤ 5 · H-bond acceptors ≤ 10. Violations predict poor absorption from the gut into the bloodstream.

Composite Score Formula (how candidates are ranked)

0.35 × DTI + 0.30 × QED + 0.20 × (1 − SAS/9) + 0.15 × Tanimoto

DTI = Drug-Target Interaction score [0–1]. Higher composite = more promising candidate. Maximum theoretical score = 1.0.

DTI Heuristic Formula (Phase 1)

0.35 × QED + 0.25 if Lipinski pass + 0.20 × LogP range bonus + 0.20 × (10−SAS)/9

LogP bonus: full 0.20 if LogP is 0–3; half (0.10) if −1 to 5; zero otherwise.

Running an Experiment — Step by Step

1
Navigate to Run Experiment

Click "Run Experiment" in the top navigation bar. You must be logged in.

2
Paste your protein target sequence

Copy one of the sequences from Section 4, or find your own at UniProt.org. Paste the raw sequence (no FASTA header, no spaces).

3
Enter your seed SMILES

Paste any valid SMILES string. A 2D structure preview renders automatically as you type, confirming the molecule is valid. Use the examples from Section 3.

4
Set the Diversity Level

Low (0.1–0.3): Small modifications — close structural analogs, high Tanimoto similarity.
Medium (0.4–0.6): Balanced — good diversity without losing relevance. Start here.
High (0.7–1.0): Large structural changes — more creative exploration, lower Tanimoto.

5
Choose candidate count and click Generate Candidates

10–20 candidates is a good starting point. Gemma4 on the DGX returns results in under 60 seconds. If generation yields fewer candidates than requested, Gemma4 retries automatically (up to 3×).

6
Add title and hypothesis, then Publish

The Save & Publish panel appears after generation. Click ✨ AI Suggest to let Gemma4 draft a title and hypothesis. Edit to match your scientific reasoning, then click Publish to Class Feed.

Publishing & Collaboration

Science advances through peer review. After generating candidates, publish your experiment to the Class Feed so classmates can review, critique, and build on your work — mirroring the real scientific process.

Publishing Workflow

1
Add a descriptive Title

Required before publishing. Include the scaffold name and the target protein. Example: "Imatinib analog search targeting BCR-ABL fusion kinase"

2
Write a scientific Hypothesis

Explain what structural modification you explored, and why it might improve binding or ADMET properties. Use ✨ AI Suggest for a Gemma4-generated draft — then edit it to reflect your actual reasoning.

3
Publish to Class Feed

Your experiment becomes visible to all students in your cohort with your name, date, and top candidate highlighted. You can retract and republish later if you revise your hypothesis.

4
Engage with comments

Respond to classmates' feedback. Strong scientific engagement is part of the grade. You can also like and comment on others' experiments.

Comment Tags — Use Them for Structured Peer Review

question

Ask for clarification. "Why did you choose diversity 0.7 rather than 0.4?"

suggestion

Propose an alternative. "Have you tried starting from Dasatinib instead of Imatinib?"

correction

Point out an error. "The BCR-ABL fragment you used is the SH3 domain, not the kinase domain."

praise

Acknowledge strong work. "Excellent insight linking SAS to synthetic feasibility."

Writing a Good Hypothesis

Template: "Starting from [seed molecule], I explored [type of structural modification] to improve [property] for the [target name]. I hypothesize that [change] will [predicted effect] because [chemical rationale]."
Strong example: "Starting from Imatinib, I explored piperazine N-substituent modifications (diversity = 0.4) targeting BCR-ABL. I hypothesize that replacing the N-methyl group with a shorter ethyl chain will reduce CYP3A4-mediated metabolism without disrupting the critical hydrogen bond to Thr315, potentially improving the drug's half-life."
Weak example: "I used the Imatinib SMILES and got some candidates. The top one has a score of 0.72." — This has no scientific reasoning and will not earn engagement from peers.
