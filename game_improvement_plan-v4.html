Docking Phase Sequence is Scientifically Accurate

Membrane → Binding → Docking mirrors real computational docking workflows. The 3-phase animation creates genuine pedagogical value.

✅

Drug Discovery Facts Carousel is Excellent

8 rotating insights with Key Term callouts. This is the strongest educational element — kids absorb it during a natural "waiting" moment.

What Needs Fixing (Critical)
❌

"EVADED!" for Low Scores Feels Like Failure

When composite score < 30%, the game says the molecule missed entirely. In real science, every test — even a failed binding — is data, not a miss.

❌

Win Condition Is Grinding, Not Discovery

Reducing 300 HP requires 12–20+ attacks at max-damage scores. The real science moment — "I found a better molecule than the known drug!" — should win immediately.

❌

Known Drug Baseline Is Hidden

GAME_KNOWN_SCORE (60%) is tracked but never prominently shown during play. Students can't feel the "improvement" if they don't see what they're improving upon.

⚠️

Boss HP Metaphor Disconnects from Science

"Reducing HP" suggests violence, not chemistry. A virus isn't defeated by HP — it's blocked when a molecule fits its active site. The metaphor leaks.

⚠️

Monochromatic Cyan-Only Theme Reduces Information Density

The dark background itself is age-appropriate for 14–18 year olds (confirmed by research — see Color tab). The real problem: only one accent colour (cyan). Every bar, label, and state competes equally. Need a semantic colour vocabulary: each feedback type gets its own distinct colour.

Where the Game's Science Is Sound
🧬

Composite Score Formula is Correct

Combining QED + synthesizability + Lipinski compliance into a composite is standard practice in real CADD pipelines.

🔗

Tanimoto Similarity for Novelty is Correct

Using Morgan fingerprints + Tanimoto < 0.4 as the novelty threshold matches ChEMBL and PubChem search standards.

⚗️

Docking Phase Labels are Meaningful

Membrane penetration → receptor binding → site docking is the actual three-stage pharmacokinetic funnel for small molecule drugs.

Where the Game's Science Diverges — and What to Fix
❌

Myth: A low-scoring molecule is a "miss" (EVADED!)

Reality: Every tested molecule generates binding data. A 45% score means partial fit — not zero information. In real lead opt, the failed candidates inform the next generation. Fix: replace "EVADED!" with "Partial binding — 45% fit. The molecule learned something." Damage = 0 but science card still shows the data.

❌

Myth: You need many molecules to win

Reality: ONE novel molecule that outperforms the known drug baseline is a publishable discovery. The win condition should be "find ONE molecule: novel + composite > known_score + 0.05" — not "grind 300 boss HP."

⚠️

Partial issue: Win threshold 72% is arbitrary

72% composite is hardcoded regardless of what the known drug scores (60%). The real target should be "beat the known drug by at least 5 points" — relative improvement, not an absolute cutoff. This is how the real experiment works.

💡

Opportunity: Novelty check should happen during play, not only at the end

When a molecule scores > baseline, show a live novelty indicator in the docking guide: "🔍 Checking ChEMBL for known analogs…" — making cross-validation part of the discovery moment rather than a post-game checklist.

Proposed New Win Condition (Science-Faithful)
"Victory when the player fires any molecule that (a) scores at least 5 percentage points above the known drug baseline AND (b) passes novelty check (Tanimoto < 0.4 against ChEMBL top-hit). Any improvement below 5% shows 'Progress! Getting closer.' Zero-damage molecules still show science data. Game over only if shield reaches 0."

This matches how the real /run-3d experiment defines success, preserves the drug-discovery integrity, and makes every high-quality shot a potential one-hit win — rewarding excellent play rather than grinding.

Win Logic Deep-Dive

The current damage formula: damage = (composite - 0.30) / 0.70 × 18 + discovery_bonus. Max damage = 28. Boss HP = 300. That means a minimum of ~11 perfect-score attacks to win — realistically 20+.

❌ CURRENT LOGIC
  • Win = reduce boss HP to 0 (300 HP tank)
  • Requires 12–20+ successful attacks
  • Same molecule can be fired repeatedly to win
  • Novelty check only runs at end-game
  • Below 30% composite = zero information shown
  • "EVADED!" feels like failure to the student
  • Known drug baseline score (60%) is invisible during play
  • Win threshold (72%) is disconnected from known score
  • Discovery moment is diluted over many rounds
✅ PROPOSED LOGIC
  • Win = 1 novel molecule that beats known baseline by +5%
  • One perfect shot can win immediately
  • Novel check happens at the discovery moment
  • Every score shown — no "EVADED," only binding %
  • "PROGRESS!" badge when you beat baseline partially
  • Known baseline shown prominently at all times
  • Win threshold = known_score + 0.05 (dynamic, per-target)
  • Boss visual = binding affinity ring, not HP bar
  • One discovery moment = clear "a-ha!" for the student

Specific UX Gaps for Students
❌

"EVADED!" Language Is Discouraging

Students who fire a molecule and see "EVADED!" think they did something wrong. They don't understand that low composite score = partial binding, not zero science. Replace with binding-percentage language.

❌

No Visible Progress Toward the Goal

The Best Score bar shows a number, but there's no clear message "you are X% away from winning." Students need a proximal goal indicator.

⚠️

Molecule Names Are Opaque

"MX-4203" doesn't teach anything. Consider showing the molecule class: "MX-4203 (Fluorinated Amide)" — even a one-word scaffold type builds chemical intuition.

⚠️

SMILES String Is Shown but Never Explained

Students see "CC1CC..." but don't know what it means. A tiny "What is SMILES?" tooltip that says "A text recipe for atoms — like DNA code for a drug" would build vocabulary.

⚠️

Combo Rewards Are Disconnected from Science

Getting a "combo" for consecutive hits is fun but doesn't reinforce why repeated improvement matters in science. Rename combos to "Optimization Streak" — same mechanic, science framing.

✅

Docking Guide Facts Are Age-Appropriate

The 8 drug discovery facts are pitched at the right level — not too simple, not too technical. The KEY TERM callout is excellent for vocabulary building.

✅

Patient Story Creates Emotional Buy-In

Starting with a patient case file is pedagogically excellent. Students who care about the patient will persist through the learning curve.

Quick UX Wins for Student Engagement
  1. Show progress as a delta: "Your best: +11% above the known drug" is more motivating than "71%".
  2. Celebrate partial success: A "Getting Warmer! 🔥" animation when score exceeds previous best by any amount.
  3. Name the science: When docking completes, say "MX-4203 achieved 71% binding affinity at the active site" — not just a number.
  4. Molecule Journey panel: Small timeline showing scores of all molecules fired this session — students see their optimization path.
  5. Hint system: After 3 low-score shots, show "Hint: Try a molecule with higher QED — increase the Drugability score." Connects score to action.

The research verdict: Your daughter's instinct is right for her age group. The bright blue game in the screenshot is appropriate for 6–9 year olds because:

  • Young children rely heavily on colour to identify objects (Fruto Design research)
  • Bright primary colours signal "safe" and "fun" for that age
  • Minimal text, maximum visual — matches cognitive level

But for 14–18 year olds, multiple verified sources confirm they actively reject bright primary colour schemes as "childish" — they want the aesthetic of Discord, Twitch, or Foldit. A sky-blue Roblox-style theme on a drug-discovery game would likely cause teens to disengage.

Critical question to answer first: What is the exact age range of the target student? This changes the entire colour recommendation. If 10–13 → softer brights. If 14+ → dark but vibrant is correct.
